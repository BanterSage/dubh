Third French Republic History

How It Began (1870)

In 1870, France was defeated by Prussia in the Franco-Prussian War. Emperor Napoleon III was captured at the Battle of Sedan.

Soon after, the empire collapsed and France declared the Third Republic in September 1870.

At first, many politicians expected the republic to be temporary, but it gradually became permanent.

Early Years and Challenges (1870s)

The new republic faced serious problems

The loss of Alsace-Lorraine to Germany

The violent uprising of the Paris Commune in 1871

Political conflict between monarchists and republicans

By 1875, constitutional laws were passed that officially established a parliamentary system with a president and two legislative chambers.

Republican Stability and Reform (1880s-1914)

Over time, the republic became more stable and strongly republican.

Major developments included:

Expansion of public education

Separation of Church and State

Growth of French colonial empire in Africa and Asia

Strengthening of democratic institutions

One major political crisis during this time wa the Drefus Affair, which deeply divided French soviety over issues of justive, antisemitism, and the power of the military.

World War I (1914-1918)

The Third Republic led France through World War I against Germany and its allies.

Although France was victorious, the war caused:

Massive casualties

Economic strain

Political tension

The postwar period was marked by rebuilding and instability.

Decline and Fall (1930s-1940)

During the Great Depression, France experienced:

Economic crisis

Political divisions

Weak coalition governments

In 1940, Germany invaded and quickly defeated France during World War II. The government collapsed, ending the Third Republic and leading to the Vichy regime.
